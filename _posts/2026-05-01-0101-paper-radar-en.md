> **TL;DR:** This round is about agents becoming trainable systems rather than prompt wrappers: live outcome rewards for forecasting agents, process rewards for data-analysis agents, visual memory for long-horizon tool use, controllable embodied world models, and a mechanistic look at why RL post-training may generalize better than SFT.

## What I Am Watching This Round

The papers I kept all sit around the same question: where does the feedback signal live? In FutureWorld, the signal is a delayed real-world outcome. In DataPRM, it is an environment-aware verifier that can inspect intermediate execution states. In OCR-Memory, it is faithful recall from long interaction histories. In the world-model paper, it is a higher-level action space that makes planning tractable. The RL-mechanisms paper then asks what post-training does inside the model when the feedback signal is RL rather than imitation.

I screened recent arXiv/Hugging Face/community signals from April 27-30, 2026, including ClawGym, GLM-5V-Turbo, X-WAM, MiniCPM-o 4.5, and several agent-security or benchmark papers. The five below were the strongest fit for this issue because I could read open full text and because each one adds something specific to the tracked Paper Radar themes.

## Paper Notes

### FutureWorld: A Live Environment for Training Predictive Agents with Real-World Outcome Rewards

**Authors:** Zhixin Han, Yanzhi Zhang, Chuyang Wei, Maohang Gao, Xiawei Yue, Kefei Chen, Yu Zhuang, Haoxiang Guan, Jiyan He, Jian Li, Yitong Duan, Yu Shi, Mengting Hu, Shuxin Zheng  
**Institutions:** Nankai University; Academy of Mathematics and Systems Science, Chinese Academy of Sciences; Zhongguancun Academy; Tsinghua University  
**Date:** arXiv, April 29, 2026  
**Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26733), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26733v1), [FutureWorld dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/PredictingFuture/FutureWorld)

![FutureWorld training loop](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26733v1/x3.png)

*Figure 3 shows the core loop: daily question release, agent search and prediction, delayed ground-truth retrieval, reward computation, and model update.*

FutureWorld reframes live forecasting as an agent training environment. Each day, the system generates prediction questions from public sources, asks an agent to search and reason before outputting probabilities, stores the rollout, then later retrieves real outcomes and assigns rewards. The interesting part is not just the benchmark. It is that search behavior and evidence use sit inside the training loop instead of being precomputed.

The authors train three open-source base models on a daily schedule with outcome-based RL. The paper reports that delayed outcomes can improve later checkpoints, though the result is uneven: one selected benchmark day shows Qwen2.5-3B improving from 0.00 to 43.72 overall, DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B from 13.57 to 34.53, while Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 drops slightly from 54.96 to 52.08. That asymmetry is useful. It keeps the claim in the realm of engineering evidence rather than a blanket promise.

What I care about is the delayed-feedback design. A forecasting agent that updates only after outcomes become real is closer to a scientific instrument than a static QA model. The weak spot is also clear: the paper reports that many questions remain unresolved at the scheduled retrieval time, and unresolved examples are discarded. That wastes rollouts and may bias learning toward fast-resolving event types. I would track whether future versions learn from longer-delayed outcomes rather than throwing them away.

**Connection to tracked themes:** agentic training, live evaluation, outcome-reward learning, continual adaptation.

### Lifting Embodied World Models for Planning and Control

**Authors:** Alex N. Wang, Trevor Darrell, Pavel Izmailov, Yutong Bai, Amir Bar  
**Institutions:** New York University; BAIR, UC Berkeley  
**Date:** arXiv, April 28, 2026  
**Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26182), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26182v1)

![Lifted World Model overview](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26182v1/x1.png)

*Figure 1 gives the key abstraction: a lightweight policy maps high-level waypoints into low-level joint actions, then the frozen world model rolls out future observations.*

This paper tackles a practical bottleneck in embodied world models: low-level action spaces are too large for efficient planning. The authors keep the base world model frozen and train a lightweight policy that maps visual high-level actions into sequences of joint actions. In their human-like embodiment setting, the high-level actions are 2D waypoints for leaf joints such as pelvis, head, and hands.

The evidence is concrete. On 128 CEM planning tasks, lifted CEM with 2D waypoints reaches 0.374 all-joint MJE, compared with 0.616 for PEVA CEM in low-level joint space. On held-out environments, lifted CEM remains better than PEVA CEM, though with some degradation. The paper also notes a limitation I find important: waypoints cannot specify goal poses fully outside the current observation, which matters if the world model eventually becomes good at imagining unseen space.

My read is that this is less about a particular waypoint interface and more about how to make world models usable for control. A world model that only accepts high-dimensional low-level controls is hard to steer. A lifted action interface gives the planner something closer to intent.

**Connection to tracked themes:** world models, embodied agents, hierarchical action spaces, planning under model constraints.

### OCR-Memory: Optical Context Retrieval for Long-Horizon Agent Memory

**Authors:** Jinze Li, Yang Zhang, Xin Yang, Jiayi Qu, Jinfeng Xu, Shuo Yang, Junhua Ding, Edith Cheuk-Han Ngai  
**Institutions:** The University of Hong Kong; University of North Texas; University of Tsukuba; Yonsei University  
**Date:** arXiv, April 29, 2026  
**Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.26622), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26622v1)

![OCR-Memory overview](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26622v1/x1.png)

*Figure 1 shows the memory pipeline: render trajectories as multi-resolution images, locate relevant marked segments, then deterministically fetch the original text.*

OCR-Memory is a clever inversion of agent memory. Instead of compressing old trajectories into summaries or embeddings, it renders interaction logs as images and trains an optical retrieval model to point to relevant marked segments. Once a segment is selected, the original text is fetched verbatim from storage. That last detail matters: the visual model does not answer the question, it selects evidence.

The experiments use Mind2Web and AppWorld. OCR-Memory improves Mind2Web element accuracy from AWM's 49.1 to 53.8 and AppWorld average success from 56.2 for ACON to 58.1. The system trade-off is explicit: it cuts text tokens per step from 3,980 for Text-RAG to 596, but increases disk per episode from 18 KB to 1.47 MB and retrieval latency from 0.3 s to 1.7 s.

I like this paper because it treats memory fidelity as a systems problem. Summaries are cheap, but they erase exact error messages, UI structure, and temporal details. OCR-Memory pays in storage and retrieval latency to keep that evidence recoverable. The main risk is deployment complexity: it requires a specialized fine-tuned optical retriever and image rendering pipeline.

**Connection to tracked themes:** document intelligence, long-horizon agents, visual retrieval, faithful memory.

### Rewarding the Scientific Process: Process-Level Reward Modeling for Agentic Data Analysis

**Authors:** Zhisong Qiu, Shuofei Qiao, Kewei Xu, Yuqi Zhu, Lun Du, Ningyu Zhang, Huajun Chen  
**Institutions:** Zhejiang University; Ant Group  
**Date:** arXiv, April 27, 2026  
**Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24198), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.24198v1), [code](https://github.com/zjunlp/DataMind)

![DataPRM framework](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.24198v1/x5.png)

*Figure 3 outlines DataPRM: diverse trajectory generation, step-level annotation, environment-aware verification, and a reflection-aware reward strategy.*

This is the data-agent paper I would prioritize from this batch. The authors argue that general process reward models fail on data analysis because they miss silent errors and penalize useful exploration. DataPRM is a 4B environment-aware generative verifier: it can interact with the code/data environment, run checks, and assign ternary feedback that separates correct steps, wrong steps, and neutral exploratory moves.

In Best-of-N test-time scaling with Qwen3-235B-A22B-Instruct as the policy, DataPRM reaches 40.89 average on DABStep at N=16, ahead of self-rewarding at 39.77 and much larger PRM baselines. In RL experiments, the paper reports gains over outcome-reward baselines on DABench and TableBench. The ablation is also readable: removing the environment, multi-turn interaction, or reflection-aware reward strategy weakens performance.

The caveat is that the verifier itself becomes an agent with tools, cost, and failure modes. But for data analysis, that may be the right direction. A reward model that cannot inspect intermediate dataframes, code outputs, or silent logical mistakes is likely too shallow for scientific workflows.

**Connection to tracked themes:** data agents, agentic training, process rewards, scientific workflow reliability.

### Why Does Reinforcement Learning Generalize? A Feature-Level Mechanistic Study of Post-Training in Large Language Models

**Authors:** Dan Shi, Zhuowen Han, Simon Ostermann, Renren Jin, Josef van Genabith, Deyi Xiong  
**Institutions:** Tianjin University; German Research Center for Artificial Intelligence; Saarland University  
**Date:** arXiv, April 27, 2026  
**Links:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.25011), [HTML](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.25011v1), [code](https://github.com/danshi777/RL-generalization)

![Feature overlap heatmaps](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.25011v1/x5.png)

*Figure 2 tracks feature overlap across checkpoints, contrasting how SFT and RL reshape internal representations during post-training.*

This paper is a mechanistic counterpart to the training papers above. The authors train SFT and RL models from the same base models on identical data, then use sparse crosscoders and a three-model attribution setup to compare internal features across base, SFT, and RL variants.

The behavioral setup matches the common observation: RL improves math and often transfers better to general tasks, while SFT can forget. For Qwen3-4B, RL beats SFT by 31.0 on CommonsenseQA and 37.7 on SciQ in the reported table. Mechanistically, the authors find that SFT introduces many specialized features early, while RL changes features more gradually and preserves more of the base representation. They then identify compact generalization-controlling features and test causality: zeroing those features in RL models hurts unseen-task performance, while amplifying them in base models improves it.

I am cautious about over-reading any single interpretability pipeline, especially when feature definitions depend on the chosen crosscoder setup. Still, the controlled comparison is valuable. It gives a more testable version of "SFT memorizes, RL generalizes": RL may be reweighting a compact shared feature set instead of installing a large task-specific representation.

**Connection to tracked themes:** large model mechanisms, post-training, mechanistic interpretability, RL generalization.

## Reading Priority And Next Questions

My priority order after this round is:

1. **FutureWorld** and **DataPRM** for agent training loops with meaningful feedback.
2. **OCR-Memory** for the practical memory bottleneck in long-horizon agents.
3. **Lifting Embodied World Models** for controllable world-model planning.
4. **RL Generalization Mechanisms** for a mechanistic hypothesis worth testing across more model families.

The next questions I would track are simple but important. Can live outcome-reward systems avoid throwing away slow-resolving examples? Can process reward models for data analysis stay reliable when the verifier itself has tool-use failures? Can visual memory beat text retrieval outside curated benchmarks once storage and latency are included? And can the RL feature-generalization story survive stronger causal tests across architectures and datasets?
